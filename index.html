Vision · Memory · Spatiotemporal Prediction

Giuliana Martinatti Giorjiani

PhD candidade in Cognitive Neuroscience

What do a tennis player and a hunting falcon have in common? Both must anticipate where a moving target will be in the near future. A tennis player doesn't swing where the ball is, but where it will be. A hunting falcon doesn't chase where its prey was a moment ago, but where it expects it to move next. Whether it's a tennis ball or fleeing prey, successful interception depends on continuously predicting the target's future trajectory. Yet moving objects are fast, and our visual experience is far from continuous. Every blink, passing obstacle, or momentary distraction interrupts the stream of visual information reaching our eyes. Despite these interruptions, the brain seamlessly reconstructs the dynamics of the world, allowing us to perceive, predict, and act with remarkable precision. How does the brain achieve this? This question has been the driving force behind much of my research. During my PhD, I have investigated how the brain predicts the future states of moving objects when visual information is unavailable, how it preserves and reconstructs visual information in memory, and which neural mechanisms support the spatiotemporal predictions that allow us to interact with a constantly changing world. I have approached these questions using a combination of behavioral experiments, functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI), eye tracking, and computational modeling

Science Beyond the Lab

A collection of projects that explore vision science outside traditional laboratory settings: from natural image datasets, to cockpit eye tracking, to playful MRI demonstrations.

Field Note 01

FiSH & CHiPs Dataset

Together with Amit Rawal, I was awarded the Tom Troscianko Grant to attend ECVP 2023, where we developed an image dataset from natural landscapes photographed across Belgium, France, England, and Scotland.

Together with Naveen Balachandran, the dataset has been curated and preprocessed, and will shortly be made publicly available for vision science community.

Field Note 02

Visual Scanning in the Cockpit

While learning how to fly, I recorded my own eye-movements in the cockpit using wearable eye tracking, capturing my erratic gaze moving between instruments, outside view, and the runway.

Field Note 03

Scanning the Unexpected

Using a 3T MRI scanner, I scanned fruits and vegetables to reveal their hidden internal structure and create cool images that make MRI images look playful, and visually engaging.
